Recipe 1

The Cosmic Distance Ladder

How to get H₀ from Supernovae Ia — the SH0ES approach

Ingredients

  • Hubble's Law: v = H₀ · d
  • Apparent magnitude m — how bright a star appears
  • Absolute magnitude M — true brightness (at 10 pc)
  • Cepheid variable stars — pulsating standard candles
  • Type Ia Supernovae — white dwarfs exploding at 1.44 M☉
  • Parallax from nearby stars
  • Redshift z from spectroscopy
Core idea: We need both velocity (easy — measure redshift) and distance (hard!) of distant galaxies. The "ladder" builds distance rung by rung.
1

Anchor: Parallax in nearby galaxies

For very close stars, measure distance d via geometric parallax — the apparent shift as Earth orbits the Sun.

Distance Modulus M = m − 5 · log₁₀(d) + 5

With d known from parallax, compute absolute magnitude M of Cepheids in that galaxy.

2

Calibrate: The Period–Luminosity Relation

Cepheids pulsate with a period P tightly correlated with luminosity:

Leavitt's Law M_Ceph = a · log₁₀(P) + b

Measure the period → know the luminosity.

3

Bridge: Cepheids + Supernova Ia in same galaxy

Find a galaxy with both Cepheids and a Type Ia SN. Cepheids give the distance. Observe the SN apparent magnitude and compute its absolute magnitude. Since all Type Ia SNe explode at the Chandrasekhar limit (1.44 M☉), they are standardizable candles.

Supernova Absolute Magnitude M_SN = m_SN − (m_Ceph − M_Ceph)
4

Measure: Distant SNe → H₀

For far galaxies with only a SN visible, use the calibrated M_SN to get distance, combine with redshift:

The Hubble Constant H₀ = c · z / d_L

Recipe 2

The Cosmic Microwave Background

How to get H₀ from the sound of the early Universe — the Planck approach

Ingredients

  • CMB — afterglow of the Big Bang (photons from recombination, z ≈ 1100)
  • ΛCDM model — standard cosmology: dark energy Λ + cold dark matter
  • Acoustic oscillations — sound waves in the primordial plasma
  • Sound horizon r_s — how far sound traveled before recombination
  • Angular diameter distance D_A — converts physical size to angle
1

The Hot Dense Plasma

Early Universe: a very dense plasma of photons, electrons, and baryons. Density fluctuations set off a tug-of-war:

• Gravity pulls matter inward (compression)

• Radiation pressure from photons pushes outward

This creates acoustic oscillations — sound waves at ~57% the speed of light.

Interactive

Acoustic Oscillations in the Primordial Plasma

A density perturbation sends out a spherical sound wave. Dark matter stays put while baryons ride the wave outward until recombination freezes it.

2

The Sound Horizon & the CMB Power Spectrum

The sound horizon r_s is the maximum distance sound traveled until recombination (~380,000 years). This imprints a characteristic angular scale:

Angular scale of the sound horizon θ_s = r_s / D_A

A harmonic analysis of the CMB temperature gives the power spectrum with peaks at multipoles:

l_n ≈ n · π / θ_s

The first peak = fundamental (one compression). Second peak = first overtone (compress + rarefy). Just like harmonics of a musical instrument.

3

From θ_s to H₀

Angular Diameter Distance D_A = (1/H₀) · ∫ dz / E(z)

Since r_s is determined by early-universe physics (well-constrained from peak ratios) and θ_s is observed, solving for D_A gives H₀.

Key insight: The CMB method is model-dependent — it requires ΛCDM and fits all parameters simultaneously. The sound horizon acts as a "standard ruler."
Recipe 3

Strong Gravitational Lensing

How to get H₀ from time delays — a one-step measurement

Ingredients

  • A multiply-imaged quasar — lensed by a foreground galaxy
  • Time delays Δt — different light travel time between images
  • A lens mass model — mass distribution of the foreground galaxy
  • Redshifts of both lens z_l and source z_s
  • Fermat potential φ — encodes lensing geometry
Why special: Unlike the distance ladder or CMB, lensing time delays give a direct, one-step measurement of H₀ independent of the distance ladder and largely independent of ΛCDM. Works at intermediate redshifts (z ~ 0.5–1.5).
1

Find a Multiply-Imaged Quasar

A massive galaxy between us and a distant quasar bends light along multiple paths. We see 2 or 4 images. Each path has a different length through curved spacetime.

2

Measure the Time Delay

Quasars flicker. Monitoring multiple images over months, we see the same variability pattern shifted in time. The delay is typically days to months.

Time Delay Δt = (D_Δt / c) · ΔΦ(Fermat potential)

3

From Time Delay to H₀

The time-delay distance:

Time-Delay Distance D_Δt = (1 + z_l) · D_l · D_s / D_ls

This is inversely proportional to H₀:

The Hubble Constant H₀ ∝ ΔΦ / (c · Δt)

Since Δt is observed and ΔΦ comes from the lens model, we get H₀ directly in physical units — no ladder needed.

Current status: TDCOSMO (Birrer et al. 2025): H₀ = 71.6 ± 3.6 km/s/Mpc. Consistent with SH0ES, in ~3σ tension with Planck. Main systematic: the mass-sheet degeneracy in the lens model.

Result: TDCOSMO (2025)

Using 8 lensed quasars with time-delay measurements, stellar kinematics to break the mass-sheet degeneracy, and hierarchical Bayesian modelling (Birrer et al. 2025):

TDCOSMO H₀
H₀ = 71.6 ± 3.6 km/s/Mpc

Consistent with SH0ES, in ~3σ tension with Planck. The dominant systematic is the mass-sheet degeneracy: Δt → (1 − κext) Δt.

So… what's the tension?

~5σ

The local measurement (SH0ES: 73.0 ± 1.0) and the early-Universe measurement (Planck: 67.4 ± 0.5) disagree at ~5σ. Either unaccounted systematics, or new physics beyond ΛCDM.
